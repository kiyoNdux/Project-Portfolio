No 3 Simple harmonic Motion

Types of Simple Harmonic Motion

  • Equilibrium position or resting position is assumed by the body when it is not vibrating (or in spring: stretching / compressing).
  • Center of oscillation is a place on the oscillating object where the mass is concentrated and the period remains constant.
  • Restoring force (-F) is the force that tends to restore a body from its displacement to its equilibrium position. In spring, it‚Äôs called spring force.
  • External force (F) or applied force can cause object, like spring, to stretch or compressed by a certain displacement x.
  • Amplitude (A) is the maximum displacement of a body from its equilibrium position.
  • Cycle is a one complete oscillation or to-andfro motion.
  • Period (T) is the time required to make a complete cycle (back-and-forth). It also depends only on the mass and the force constant. The greater the mass (m), the longer the period. The stiffer the spring, the shorter the period.
  • Frequency (f) is the number of the oscillations or cycle per unit time. The SI unit for frequency is Hertz or Hz (1 Hz = 1 cycle per second or 1s-1 ).
  • Angular frequency (œâ) measures angular displacement per unit time. It is commonly express in radians per second.

No 5 Sound And Wave

Sound -is a form of energy and a mechanical wave that cannot travel through a vacuum. It needs a material medium (like solid, liquid or gas) for their propagation. When sound waves are represented in a waveform, we instantly notice some basic characteristics.

  • Amplitude - refers to the distance of the maximum vertical displacement of the wave from its mean position. It also refers to the volume of sounds. Its SI unit is given by meters (m).
  • Wavelength - is the distance between two consecutive peaks in a sound wave. Its SI unit is given by meters (m). It is represented by the symbol Œª (lambda)
  • Frequency - refers to the rate of the vibration of the sound travelling through the air. It is also known as Pitch.
  • Speed -like all waves, a sound travels at a certain speed. Its speed in a medium is affected by the medium‚Äôs rigidity (or compressibility in gases), density, and temperature. The speed of sound in air is low, because air is compressible, and is greater in liquids and solids, because these are relatively rigid and very difficult to compress.
